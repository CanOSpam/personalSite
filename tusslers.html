Tumbleweed Tusslers

Tumbleweed Tusslers was my greatest challenge to date. My class was tasked by our professor, to "Make a linux game with a large networking component." That was, more or less, the extent of our guidance. A friend and I led 20 of our peers over the course of four months to make that game. We soon settled on a 2D, western themed, last man standing style game since we want to be on trend, of course. Over the next approximately three and a half months, six teams worked hard to create a game that could handle 25 simultaneous players with next to no latency. The game has a shrinking danger zone that deals damage over time, four different player equippable weapons, and a great 2.5D art style. I'm proud of the people that poured countless hours into this game and I'm happy to include it in my portfolio.

 

We built Tumbleweed Tusslers on the back of Unity which, despite the various fun issues it presented us with, ended up being a great tool to quickly build out a game with. After much testing our server had to built solely in C# due to performance issues, and had we had more time, likely would have been ported to C++ like our networking library.

 

Our networking library used a combination of TCP and UDP. Map data and initial spawn locations were delivered up front using TCP to prevent any data loss. Once the game started the clients and the server switch to a custom UDP protocol that uses hand crafted packets that we laboured over for the course of the term. You can check out a blog post I wrote about those packets and how we came up with them here.

 

At the end of the day, Tumbleweed Tusslers was a great learning experience for all of us that worked on it. We learned a whole lot about collaborating on large teams and how to keep up morale in the face of an occasionally overwhelming workload. Plus, making a custom networking protocol and client-server architecture turned out to be a pretty dang fun challenge.
